## Design Context

### Users
Behavioral-science researchers using jsPsych. Their context when opening this tool: a participant's data looked off, or a trial behaved unexpectedly, and they need to see exactly what happened on screen. They are debugging — focused, technical, looking for evidence. They are not browsing for fun. The tool succeeds when it disappears and they trust what they're seeing.

The job: load a recording, find the trial in question, watch it play back, read the trial data, draw a conclusion.

### Brand Personality
**Precise · restrained · forensic.** Closer to a scientific instrument than to a product. The voice is plainspoken and direct — "Load Recording", "Trial 3", "0.4s / 4.2s" — never marketing. No exclamation points, no encouragement, no personality flourishes. The tool defers to the data.

Emotional goal: confidence. The researcher should feel they're using something built by someone who took the data seriously.

### Aesthetic Direction
Refined minimalism. Near-monochrome surface with a single restrained accent used only to convey state (active trial, cursor position, the playhead). Light theme; the replay stage has a hard, intentional boundary against the surrounding chrome so the researcher always knows what is participant content and what is tool. The stage is sacrosanct — chrome never competes with it.

Typography-led hierarchy: size, weight, and spacing carry the structure. No decorative dividers, drop shadows, gradients, glow, or glassmorphism.

**Anti-references** (avoid all of these):
- AI-startup look — cyan-on-dark, purple/blue gradients, neon glow, glassmorphism, gradient text on metrics
- Toy aesthetic — bright primaries, cartoon icons, rounded-everything
- Corporate/SaaS — Inter + brand-blue, identical card grids, stock-dashboard chrome, hero-metric layouts
- Noisy chrome — decorative dividers, drop shadows, anything that doesn't earn its place

### Design Principles
1. **The stage is sacrosanct.** The replay iframe is the subject under investigation. The surrounding chrome must visually subordinate to it — never compete in saturation, never bleed into it. A clear, intentional boundary separates tool from participant content.
2. **Color carries meaning, not personality.** Near-monochrome palette tinted subtly. One accent, used only for state: which trial is active, where the cursor is, where the playhead sits. If color is decorative, remove it.
3. **Typography does the structural work.** Hierarchy comes from size, weight, and spacing — not from boxes, borders, or dividers. Choose a body face that's comfortable for dense data; pair with a quiet display face for headings.
4. **Density without noise.** Researchers want information per pixel; they don't want decoration. Every line, rule, and pixel of padding must earn its place. When in doubt, remove it.
5. **Plainspoken labels.** Microcopy is literal and tight. "Load Recording", "Trial 3 · stroop", "0.4 / 4.2 s". Never market the tool to its own user.
